Word embeddings are numerical representations of words that allow software to compare language with arithmetic. Earlier text systems often used one-hot vectors or bag-of-words counts, where every word was a separate dimension and most values were zero. Those representations are easy to build, but they do not know that "car" and "automobile" are related. A dense embedding solves that problem by placing words with similar usage patterns near one another in a continuous vector space. Word2Vec made this idea famous by training a small neural network on a prediction task: either predicting a word from its neighbors or predicting neighboring words from a center word. The training task was simple, but the vectors learned useful semantic structure.

The most memorable demonstration is the analogy king - man + woman is close to queen. The equation is not magic and it is not always reliable, but it shows that some directions in the vector space can represent relationships such as gender, tense, country, or profession. GloVe, another influential method, learned embeddings from global word co-occurrence statistics rather than a local prediction window. Both approaches are based on the distributional hypothesis: words that appear in similar contexts tend to have similar meanings. This is why embeddings are often described as a bridge between human language and machine computation.

Cosine similarity is one of the most common ways to compare embeddings. It measures the angle between two vectors rather than their raw length. This matters because two text vectors can point in the same semantic direction even if one has a larger magnitude. In search systems, recommendation systems, and clustering, cosine similarity lets us rank items by meaning instead of exact string overlap. If a user searches for "fast local vector index," an embedding model can retrieve text about "approximate nearest neighbor search" even when the words are not identical.

Word embeddings also have limitations. A classic Word2Vec vector gives one representation per word, so the word "bank" has the same vector in "river bank" and "investment bank." Contextual models such as BERT later addressed this by producing different token embeddings depending on the sentence. Sentence embeddings go one step further by representing an entire sentence, paragraph, or query as one dense vector. This is especially useful for semantic search because the unit we compare is usually not a single word but a complete question or document chunk.

In a retrieval pipeline, embeddings are created twice with slightly different intent. Document chunks are embedded as searchable content, while user queries are embedded as search queries. Modern embedding APIs often expose these as different input types because documents and queries have different styles. The central idea stays the same: similar meanings should be nearby in vector space. Once text becomes vectors, the machine can sort, cluster, compare, and retrieve language at scale.

For beginners, the most important intuition is progression. Bag-of-words counts exact terms but loses order and meaning. TF-IDF improves counts by giving rare, informative words more weight. Word embeddings learn dense semantic positions for individual words. Sentence embeddings represent a complete thought. Each step solves a problem from the previous step, and each step makes language more useful for computation. This is the foundation underneath semantic search, retrieval augmented generation, and many recommendation systems.

Dense vectors are not human-readable explanations. A single dimension rarely means one clean concept, and inspecting a vector directly is usually not helpful. Instead, practitioners test embeddings by comparing nearest neighbors and measuring retrieval quality. If questions about Word2Vec consistently retrieve chunks about vector databases, the embedding model or chunking strategy is not working for the task. Good systems treat embeddings as a tool to be evaluated, not as a guarantee of understanding.

The same embedding space can support many simple operations. We can find nearest neighbors, group similar documents, remove duplicates, recommend related articles, or detect when a new question is outside the scope of a corpus. The reason these applications feel different to users is the interface around the vectors, not the core mathematical idea. Under the surface, the system is usually converting text into dense vectors and comparing them with a similarity measure such as cosine similarity.
