Space telescopes are observatories placed above most or all of Earth's atmosphere. The atmosphere blocks some wavelengths of light, blurs images through turbulence, and adds background noise. By observing from space, telescopes can collect ultraviolet, X-ray, gamma-ray, infrared, and precise visible-light data that would be difficult or impossible from the ground. Different space telescopes are designed for different wavelengths, so they often answer different scientific questions.

The Hubble Space Telescope is one of the best-known space observatories. Launched in 1990, it observes mostly visible and ultraviolet light with some near-infrared capability. Hubble's early images were blurred because of a flaw in its primary mirror, but servicing missions installed corrective optics and upgraded instruments. Astronaut repair made Hubble more capable over time. Its observations helped measure the expansion rate of the universe, study distant galaxies, observe star-forming regions, and capture detailed images of planets and nebulae.

The Chandra X-ray Observatory studies high-energy phenomena. X-rays from space are mostly absorbed by Earth's atmosphere, so they require space-based detectors. Chandra observes hot gas around galaxy clusters, supernova remnants, neutron stars, black holes, and energetic jets. X-ray astronomy reveals violent and extremely hot environments that visible-light telescopes cannot fully explain. A galaxy cluster may look calm in optical images while X-ray data shows gas heated to millions of degrees.

The Spitzer Space Telescope observed infrared light before its mission ended. Infrared observatories can study cool objects, dust clouds, star-forming regions, and distant galaxies. Spitzer helped scientists detect exoplanet atmospheres, map dusty structures, and observe objects too cold or hidden for visible-light instruments. Its mission prepared the way for later infrared observatories, including JWST, by showing how valuable space-based infrared data could be.

The Kepler Space Telescope was designed to find exoplanets using the transit method. It watched the brightness of many stars and looked for tiny, repeated dips caused by planets crossing in front of their stars. Kepler revealed that planets are common in the galaxy and that many solar systems look different from our own. Its discoveries changed astronomy by turning exoplanets from rare curiosities into a major field of study. The mission also produced data for stellar physics because it measured star brightness with great precision.

Space telescopes often work together. A discovery in one wavelength can trigger follow-up observations in another. For example, an X-ray source might be studied with optical and infrared telescopes to identify its host galaxy. A planet candidate found by transit surveys can be followed by spectroscopy. Multi-wavelength astronomy helps scientists build a fuller picture because no single telescope sees every physical process.

Engineering constraints shape every space telescope. The mirror size, orbit, detectors, cooling system, pointing stability, data rate, and launch vehicle all influence what science is possible. Some telescopes are placed in low Earth orbit, where servicing may be possible but Earth blocks part of the sky. Others are sent to more distant orbits for thermal stability and continuous viewing. The best design depends on the wavelength, mission lifetime, budget, and scientific goals.

For students learning retrieval, space telescopes provide a useful contrast between similar entities. Hubble, Chandra, Spitzer, Kepler, and JWST are all telescopes, but they are not interchangeable. A good retriever should distinguish X-ray observations from infrared observations, exoplanet transit searches from galaxy imaging, and repairable low-Earth-orbit missions from distant observatories. If chunks are too large, these distinctions may blur together. If chunks are too small, the system may miss the context that explains why each telescope matters.
