The James Webb Space Telescope, or JWST, is a large infrared observatory designed to study the universe from a position near the Sun-Earth L2 point. It is often described as a successor to the Hubble Space Telescope, but it observes mostly in infrared wavelengths rather than visible and ultraviolet light. Infrared astronomy is valuable because it can reveal cool objects, dust-covered regions, and very distant galaxies whose light has been stretched by the expansion of the universe.

JWST has a segmented primary mirror made of eighteen hexagonal beryllium mirror segments coated with gold. The mirror had to fold inside the launch vehicle and then unfold in space. A tennis-court-sized sunshield protects the telescope from heat and light from the Sun, Earth, and Moon. The sunshield has five thin layers, each helping radiate heat away. Keeping the instruments cold is essential because warm objects emit infrared radiation that would interfere with observations.

The telescope launched in December 2021 and went through a complex deployment sequence. Engineers unfolded the solar array, high-gain antenna, sunshield supports, sunshield layers, secondary mirror, and primary mirror wings. Each step had to work because JWST was too far away for astronaut repair. After deployment, the mirror segments were aligned with extreme precision so they could act as one large mirror. This commissioning period turned a folded spacecraft into a working observatory.

JWST's instruments include NIRCam, NIRSpec, MIRI, and FGS/NIRISS. NIRCam is used for near-infrared imaging and wavefront sensing. NIRSpec can take spectra of many objects at once, allowing scientists to study chemical composition, temperature, and motion. MIRI observes mid-infrared light and is useful for cooler objects and dusty environments. FGS helps with precise pointing, while NIRISS supports specialized observing modes. Together, these instruments make JWST a flexible observatory rather than a single-purpose camera.

One major science goal is to study the early universe. Because light takes time to travel, observing very distant galaxies lets astronomers look back toward the first few hundred million years after the Big Bang. JWST can detect faint infrared light from early galaxies and help measure how quickly stars, black holes, and heavy elements formed. These observations test models of cosmic history and galaxy evolution.

JWST also studies star and planet formation. Stars are born inside clouds of gas and dust that visible light often cannot penetrate. Infrared light can pass through some of that dust, letting the telescope observe young stars, protoplanetary disks, and jets. The telescope can also study exoplanet atmospheres when a planet passes in front of its star. By measuring tiny changes in starlight, scientists can infer molecules such as water vapor, carbon dioxide, methane, or clouds in an atmosphere.

The telescope is a lesson in engineering constraints. Its mirror, sunshield, instruments, orbit, and deployment plan were all connected. The observatory needed to be large enough to collect faint light, cold enough for infrared work, compact enough to launch, and stable enough for precise pointing. Those requirements made JWST expensive and technically difficult, but they also enabled observations that were not possible before.

Like all observatories, JWST does not answer questions alone. Scientists propose observations, analyze data, compare models, and publish interpretations. The telescope provides high-quality evidence, but the meaning comes from careful measurement and debate. In a retrieval analogy, JWST is not the final answer generator; it is a powerful instrument for gathering relevant context from the universe.

JWST data is archived so researchers around the world can reuse observations for new questions. This makes the telescope more valuable over time. A single exposure might support studies of galaxy structure, star formation, calibration, and background objects. The observatory therefore produces both immediate discoveries and a long-term knowledge base for astronomy.
