Mars rovers are robotic vehicles designed to explore the surface of Mars. They extend the reach of landers by driving to rocks, craters, slopes, and soil deposits that cannot be studied from one fixed location. Rovers carry cameras, spectrometers, drills, weather sensors, and sometimes sample handling systems. Because radio signals between Earth and Mars can take many minutes to travel, rovers cannot be driven like remote-control cars. Engineers send planned commands, and the rover executes them with onboard autonomy and safety checks.

Sojourner, part of the Mars Pathfinder mission in 1997, was the first successful rover on Mars. It was small, but it proved that mobile exploration could work on another planet. Spirit and Opportunity arrived in 2004 and greatly expanded the idea. Spirit studied volcanic rocks and signs of past water activity. Opportunity explored Meridiani Planum and found strong evidence that liquid water once affected the environment. Opportunity was designed for a short mission but operated for nearly fifteen years, becoming one of NASA's most durable explorers.

Curiosity landed in Gale crater in 2012 using a sky crane system that lowered the rover on cables during the final descent. Its mission was to determine whether Mars ever had conditions suitable for microbial life. Curiosity found ancient streambeds, clay minerals, and chemical evidence that Gale crater once contained habitable environments. The rover also measured radiation, weather, and atmospheric chemistry, helping engineers plan for future human missions.

Perseverance landed in Jezero crater in 2021. Jezero was selected because orbital images showed a preserved river delta, a place where water once carried sediment into a lake. Deltas on Earth are good at preserving organic material and fine layers of rock, so Jezero is a strong target for astrobiology. Perseverance studies geology, searches for signs of ancient microbial life, and collects rock cores in sealed sample tubes. These tubes are intended for a future sample return campaign.

Perseverance also carried Ingenuity, a small helicopter built to test powered flight in the thin Martian atmosphere. Ingenuity was originally a technology demonstration, but it flew many more times than expected and scouted terrain for the rover. Its success showed that aerial vehicles could support future planetary exploration. Flying on Mars is difficult because the atmosphere is thin, so rotor blades must spin very fast while the aircraft remains lightweight.

Rover missions depend on energy and mobility. Some rovers use solar panels, which can be limited by dust and seasons. Curiosity and Perseverance use radioisotope power systems, allowing them to operate through dust storms and winter conditions. Wheels must survive sharp rocks, sand, and repeated stress. Navigation software must avoid hazards while still making progress toward science targets. Every meter of travel is planned with a balance between safety and discovery.

The scientific value of rovers comes from context. A laboratory instrument can measure a rock, but the rover also photographs where the rock sits, how layers are arranged, and what nearby features suggest about the ancient environment. This context helps scientists reconstruct whether water, wind, volcanoes, or impacts shaped a location. Rovers therefore act as field geologists, moving through landscapes and building an evidence-based story over time.

Mars rovers are also examples of long-running autonomous systems. They operate with limited bandwidth, delayed commands, harsh temperatures, dust, radiation, and mechanical wear. Engineers must monitor health, update software, choose routes, and prioritize science goals. A rover mission is not a single event but a continuous retrieval and decision process: gather observations, select the most valuable targets, and adapt the plan as new evidence appears.

Public rover images also make Mars exploration easier to understand. Students can see wheel tracks, layered rocks, dust, and distant crater rims rather than reading only mission summaries. That visual record helps connect engineering decisions to scientific questions. A route is not just a path across a map; it is a sequence of evidence-gathering choices.
