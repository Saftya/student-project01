The International Space Station, often called the ISS, is a large spacecraft assembled in low Earth orbit. It serves as a laboratory, observatory, engineering testbed, and home for rotating crews. The station is a partnership involving NASA, Roscosmos, ESA, JAXA, CSA, and many contractors and research teams. Its first modules were launched in 1998, and continuous human presence began in 2000. Since then, astronauts and cosmonauts have lived and worked aboard the station for months at a time.

The ISS is built from modules, trusses, solar arrays, radiators, docking ports, robotic arms, and life-support systems. Pressurized modules provide living and working space. Large solar arrays generate electrical power, while radiators remove heat. Visiting vehicles deliver crew, food, equipment, experiments, and replacement parts. Some spacecraft stay docked as emergency return vehicles. The station must constantly balance power, thermal control, air revitalization, water recovery, communications, and orbital maintenance.

Microgravity research is one of the station's main purposes. Experiments study how fluids behave without normal convection, how flames burn differently, how materials form, and how biological systems respond to weightlessness. Human research is especially important because long missions affect muscles, bones, eyesight, balance, sleep, and immune function. This knowledge supports planning for future missions to the Moon and Mars, where crews will spend long periods away from Earth.

The ISS is also used for Earth observation. Astronauts photograph storms, fires, coastlines, cities, glaciers, and nighttime lights. Instruments mounted outside the station monitor atmosphere, vegetation, lightning, cosmic particles, and other phenomena. The orbit covers much of the inhabited planet, allowing repeated views over time. These observations connect spaceflight to climate science, disaster response, agriculture, and education.

Robotics are essential on the station. Canadarm2, the large Canadian robotic arm, helps capture cargo vehicles, move equipment, and support maintenance. Dextre, a smaller robotic system, performs more precise tasks. Robotics reduce the need for spacewalks, which are risky and time-consuming. When astronauts do perform spacewalks, they repair hardware, install upgrades, route cables, and maintain external experiments. Every spacewalk requires extensive planning, tools, safety checks, and coordination with mission control.

The station demonstrates international cooperation in a technical environment. Partners contribute modules, launch services, crew training, control centers, experiments, and funding. Political relationships on Earth can be complicated, but the station requires daily operational coordination. Shared procedures, common interfaces, and trust between teams are as important as hardware. This makes the ISS a case study in systems engineering and diplomacy.

Living on the ISS is both ordinary and unusual. Crew members exercise daily to reduce muscle and bone loss. They eat packaged food, sleep in small crew quarters, communicate with family, perform maintenance, and run experiments. Water is recycled, air is filtered, and supplies are tracked carefully. Small details matter because the station is a closed environment where waste, leaks, equipment failures, or schedule overload can affect safety and morale.

The ISS also prepares engineers for future exploration. Technologies for life support, docking, robotics, spacesuits, medical monitoring, and operations are tested in orbit before they are used farther away. Low Earth orbit is close compared with the Moon or Mars, but it is still a challenging environment. The station's long history provides data about how complex spacecraft age, how crews adapt, and how international teams manage continuous operations.

Students can think of the ISS as a constantly running experiment platform. New questions arrive, old equipment needs maintenance, and every activity competes for crew time, power, storage, and communication bandwidth. The station shows that exploration is not only about launch day. It is also about sustained operations, measurement, repair, and learning from a living system.
